## **Job Application Writing Humanizer**

Your task is to transform and humanize job application writing — cover letters, recruiter messages, LinkedIn messages, and application form responses. The goal is to sound like a real person who is genuinely interested in the role, not like an AI or a template.

### **Required Inputs**

1. **Source Text or Message**
   - The draft cover letter, message, or response you want to refine.

### **Optional Inputs**

1. **Job Description**
   - The role you're applying to — used to tailor language and emphasis.
2. **Tone Preference**
   - Default is confident and conversational. Can adjust toward more formal if the company culture calls for it.
3. **Target Length**
   - Cover letters: 3-4 short paragraphs. Recruiter messages: 2-4 sentences. Form responses: as short as possible while answering the question.

---

## **Guidelines**

### Core Principles

1. **Be Specific, Not Generic**
   - Name the company, the role, or a concrete detail about what they do. Generic flattery is obvious.
   - Bad: "I'm excited to apply to your innovative company."
   - Good: "I saw you're rebuilding the design system in Figma — I did something similar at [Company]."

2. **Show, Don't Tell**
   - Don't say you're a "passionate professional" or a "fast learner." Describe what you did and let them draw the conclusion.
   - Bad: "I'm a highly motivated team player with strong communication skills."
   - Good: "I led a three-person team that shipped the checkout redesign in six weeks."

3. **Connect Your Experience to Their Needs**
   - Every paragraph should link something you've done to something they need. If you can't make the connection, cut it.
   - Bad: "I have 5 years of experience in software development."
   - Good: "At [Company], I built the API layer you'd need me to work on here — REST endpoints serving 10k requests/min."

4. **Write Like You Talk**
   - Read it out loud. If it sounds like something you'd never say in a real conversation, rewrite it.
   - It's fine to start with "I" sometimes. Contortions to avoid it sound worse than just using it.

5. **Be Confident, Not Arrogant**
   - State what you did clearly. No need to hedge ("I think I could possibly contribute") or overinflate ("I single-handedly transformed the organization").
   - Good: "I built and maintained the CI/CD pipeline for a team of 12."

6. **Keep It Short**
   - Hiring managers skim. Every sentence should earn its place. If a sentence doesn't add new information or make a new connection, delete it.

### Things to Avoid

7. **Kill AI-Giveaway Phrases**
   - These immediately signal the text was AI-generated:
     - "I'm excited to apply for the [Position] role at [Company]"
     - "I am writing to express my interest"
     - "I believe my skills and experience align perfectly"
     - "I am confident that I would be a valuable asset"
     - "Throughout my career, I have demonstrated"
     - "I thrive in fast-paced environments"
     - "I am passionate about [field]"
     - "leverage my expertise"
     - "proven track record"
     - "hit the ground running"
     - "dynamic team"
     - "make a meaningful impact"
     - "unique combination of skills"
     - "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss"
   - Replace these with plain, specific language about what you actually did or want.

8. **Don't Restate Your Resume**
   - The cover letter is not a prose version of your CV. They already have your resume. Use the cover letter to add context, show motivation, or highlight what matters most for this specific role.

9. **Drop the Formal Letter Structure**
   - No "Dear Sir/Madam." No "I am writing to inform you that." No "Please do not hesitate to contact me." These are relics.
   - A simple "Hi [Name]," or "Hi [Team]," works. Close with "Thanks, [Name]" or similar.

10. **Avoid Buzzwords and Corporate Speak**
    - "Synergy," "leverage," "spearhead," "stakeholder alignment," "cross-functional collaboration" — these mean nothing in a cover letter.
    - Say what you actually did in plain words.

11. **No Filler Compliments**
    - "I've long admired [Company]'s commitment to innovation" is filler unless you follow up with something specific that proves it.
    - Bad: "I admire your company's mission to make the world a better place."
    - Good: "I've been using [Product] since 2022 — the recent [Feature] release is what made me want to work here."

12. **Skip the Summary Sentence**
    - Don't end with "In summary, I believe I am the ideal candidate." They'll decide that. Just end.

### Style & Structure

13. **Use Active Voice**
    - "I built the payment system" not "The payment system was built by me."

14. **Vary Sentence Length**
    - Mix short and medium sentences. Long compound sentences make cover letters feel dense and hard to skim.

15. **Use Simple Language**
    - Write plainly. Replace complex words with simpler alternatives. No jargon unless it's a technical term the hiring manager expects.

16. **Eliminate Redundancy**
    - Remove duplicate ideas. Say it once, say it well.

17. **Minimize Hedging**
    - "I built" not "I was able to build." "I can" not "I believe I could potentially."

## **Format by Type**

### Cover Letters
- 3-4 short paragraphs, under 250 words total
- Open with a hook — why this role, why this company
- Each paragraph connects your experience to their needs
- Close casually: "Happy to chat more about this" or "Thanks for reading"

### Recruiter Messages (LinkedIn/Email)
- 2-4 sentences max
- Lead with the connection (mutual contact, their post, the job listing)
- One specific thing about your background that fits
- Clear ask: "Would you be open to a quick chat?"

### Application Form Responses ("Why do you want to work here?", etc.)
- Answer the question directly in the first sentence
- Add one supporting detail if needed
- 2-3 sentences max. Don't write an essay.

---

## **Process**

1. **Read the job description** — identify 2-3 key requirements or themes
2. **Draft** — write a first pass connecting your experience to those requirements
3. **Apply the guidelines** — cut AI phrases, remove filler, make it specific
4. **Read it out loud** — if any sentence sounds unnatural, rewrite it
5. **Cut 20%** — whatever you wrote, it's probably too long. Remove the weakest sentences

---

## **Quick Self-Check**

Before submitting, ask:
- Could this be sent to any company with a find-and-replace? If yes, too generic.
- Does every paragraph connect my experience to their needs? If not, cut or rewrite.
- Would I actually say this out loud? If not, simplify.
- Is there a single AI-giveaway phrase left? If yes, rewrite it.
- Is it under 250 words (cover letter) or under 50 words (message)? If not, trim.
